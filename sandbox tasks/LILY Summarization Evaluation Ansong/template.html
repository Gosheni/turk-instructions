Summary

Criminal and terrorist organizations use TBML to disguise the origins of billions of dollars in funds generated by their illicit activities. TBML schemes can involve misrepresenting the price, quantity, or type of goods or services in trade transactions. The U.S. government, international bodies, and knowledgeable sources have recommended various practices that countries could adopt to detect and combat TBML. GAO was asked to review the use of TBML in criminal and terrorist operations. This report examines (1) the extent to which criminal andterrorist organizations useTBML to launder funds and the types of goods and services they use, (2) U. S. agencies' efforts to partner with partner countries and international bodies in combating TBML, and (3) the amount of money laundered through TBML globally. To address these objectives, GAO reviewed relevant documents and interviewed officials from the Department of Homeland Security's (DHS) Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), Department of Justice's (DOJ), and Department of State's (State) Office of International Narcotics and Law Enforcement (INA) and Office of the United Nations Office of Drugs and Crime (UNODC) and the World Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (WCO), among others. Criminal and terrorist groups use a variety of schemes to laundering the proceeds of their illicit operations and in some cases they may enlist the services of professional money laundering services. For example, criminal organizations in Colombia have used TBML for decades to disguise proceeds from illegal drug sales. In addition to drug trafficking, TBML has been used in schemes involving illegal mining, human trafficking, and the sale of counterfeit goods, among other things. Although various observers believe the magnitude ofTBML is large, specific estimates are unavailable. A number of academic studies have sought to quantify various aspects of illicit financial flows and money laundering. Although the results of such studies can shed light on the potential volume of TB ML, none of those GAO identified in its literature review sought to develop estimates of TBml specifically. The studies we reviewed all had certain methodological limitations. Available evidence from the U.N. and other sources suggests that TBML is likely one of the largest forms of money laundering and has increased in recent years. In many instances, these organizations combine TBML techniques with other forms of Money Laundering, such as bulk cash smuggling and the laundering of funds through the banking system. The United States has worked with international bodies to develop anti-money laundering standards, share information regarding TBML methods and specific cases, and provide training and technical assistance to strengthen the ability of countries to combat TB ML. For instance, Treasury's Office of Terrorist Financing and Financial Crimes heads the United States delegation to FATF, which sets internationally recognized standards for developing AML and countering the financing of terrorism (CFT) regimes and assesses member jurisdictions' ability to meet these standards. Treasury also works with its fellow FIUs in the Egmont Group to exchange tactical, operational, and strategic information to assist in efforts to combat money laundering, including TBML; however, Treasury has not provided any TBML-specific training for FIUs. U.s. agencies, including DHS, DOJ, State, and Treasury and their component agencies and offices, have taken steps to Partner with countries and International Bodies to Combat TBML but opportunities exist to enhance the TTU program. DHS, Treasury, and State provide a range of support to partner countries related to combating TB ML including funding training, technical assistance, law enforcement cooperation, and information-sharing methods. The primary partnership program is HSI's Trade-Based Money laundering (TTU) program, which is focused on combating TBml. Under this program, HSI and its partner countries exchange trade data and work together to better identify anomalies in trade data that may indicateTBML. Although HSI has shared and analyzed data with partner TTUs, the program has faced various challenges, including insufficient resources or support for the TTUs and a slow expansion of the program. HSI officials stated that they have not taken key management steps that could help guide its efforts, including developing a strategy and a performance monitoring framework. By developing a Strategy and a Performance Monitoring Framework, which would require an investment of resources, a strategy would help ensure HSI is utilizing its limited resources effectively to achieve TTU goals over the long term.
